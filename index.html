simulating deformables

during my msc in applied mechanics, a big project of mine was exploring the craftsmanship of deformable simulations in the entertainment industry.

of course, i started with the easy stuff - linear models like this - but quickly moved further to the field of nonlinear finite element method and continuum mechanics. i ended up implementing the hyperelastic projected newton solver by pixar, presented here.

here is a video;

you can get a wip version of my thesis here,

and a highly wip version of my c++/directx 12 simulator here.

any feedback is more than welcome at here.

in a nutshell, if you want compute physics, you need to find some state variables characterizing the system's response - i.e. how much energy (\(\Psi\)) is accumulated in a small cube of material if we squish it to be half the size it was before.

if you apply some external forces \(\mathbf{f}_{ext}\) on your deformable, in turn, internal forces \(\mathbf{f}_{int}\) appear inside of it, which is calculated from $$\mathbf{f}_{int} = \frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial\mathbf{x}}$$ so easy, just take the derivative! the internal forces equal the energy jacobian. you have now a nice equilibrium $$\mathbf{f}_{int}-\mathbf{f}_{ext} = \mathbf{0}$$ you can timestep forward in time this equilibrium by plugging it into a newton solver; however it means that you need to take the derivative again, which is going to be the energy hessian, that is $$\mathbf{K} = \frac{\partial^2\Psi}{\partial\mathbf{x}^2}$$ yes, just take the derivative; again! however, there's an evil professor trick lurking here: \(\mathbf{K}\) is a fourth-order tensor, and some instabilities can appear if try to e.g. squish the little cube too fast, or squish it to the point it becomes a pancake.

following smith et al., i went through the painstaking analysis of some specific energy functions to find out that you can also reassemble the whole energy hessian \(\mathbf{K}\) from the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of it (as \(\mathbf{K}=\mathbf{Q\Lambda Q}^T\)) which can be aquired in closed form!

this is so cool, because the instabilities appear as \(\mathbf{K}\) becomes indefinite, so we need to just clamp the eigenvalue to \(0\) if it's less than \(0\).

man! all of this might sound suspiciously intellectual for you, but i just copied the equations from the pixar paper and it worked. :)
