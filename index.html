simulating deformables

during my msc studies, a big project of mine was exploring the craftsmanship of high-performance deformable object simulator programming.

of course, i started with the easy stuff - linear models like this - but quickly moved further to the field of nonlinear finite element method and continuum mechanics.

in my thesis (what you can read here) i present a self-contained, unified method to simulate deformable objects, mostly inspired by the work of smith et al.


here is a very short video about the simulator:

while here you can watch the full presentation about how it works.

a highly wip version of the source code is available at github.com/marcisolti/ifem-dx12.

cool thing about this hessian projection mess is that even my crappy naïve implementation runs real-time :3 as it allows pretty large time-steps.


in a nutshell: the tricky thing is when you need to take the second derivative of the energy function \(\Psi\) to aquire the tangent stiffness matrix \(\mathbf{K}\) as $$\mathbf{K} = \frac{\partial^2\Psi}{\partial\mathbf{x}^2}$$

deriving these hessians is already pretty painful, but the really nasty stuff happens when severe deformations introduce indefiniteness to the system, which causes the simulator to explode.

i went through the painstaking analysis of some specific energy functions to find out that you can get the closed form, analytic eigenvalues \(\mathbf{\Lambda}\) and eigenvectors \(\mathbf{Q}\) of \(\mathbf{K}\) for many energy functions; then, we can reassamble the hessian from the ubiquitous $$\mathbf{K}=\mathbf{Q\Lambda Q}^T.$$ while clamping the negative eigenvalues to \(0\).
